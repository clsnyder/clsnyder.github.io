Understanding Bond Interest Rate Risk

The Inverse Relationship

The most critical concept in bond investing is the inverse relationship between a bond's market price and prevailing interest rates. When new bonds offer higher yields, existing bonds with lower fixed rates become less attractive, causing their market price to fall.

Illustrates how a bond's price changes as market interest rates fluctuate.

Scenario: A Bond's Value Drop

Imagine you buy a 5-year, $100 bond with an 8% coupon. If market interest rates rise to 9%, your bond is no longer worth $100. Its price must drop to offer a competitive yield to new investors. The new market value is the present value of all its future payments, discounted at the new 9% rate.

Strategies for Diversifying Concentrated Stock

Holding a large, highly appreciated stock position creates significant risk. Here are strategies to diversify while managing tax consequences.

Charitable Giving Strategies

Use philanthropy to unlock value. Donating appreciated stock to a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) or a Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) allows for the sale of the asset with no capital gains tax, providing significant tax deductions and a way to fund charitable goals.

Tax-Deferred & Hedging Strategies

These advanced strategies aim to defer taxes or protect against downside risk. They range from private Exchange Funds to complex structured products and options strategies like collars.

Exchange Funds

Pool stock with others to diversify, but funds are illiquid for ~7 years.

Borrowing (Margin Loans)

Access cash without selling, but carries margin call risk.

Protective Options

Use puts and calls (collars) to hedge value against market drops.

Direct Sale & Tax Planning

The most straightforward approach is to gradually sell the stock over time. This provides immediate liquidity for diversification but also triggers immediate capital gains taxes. This can be paired with tax-loss harvesting to offset gains.
